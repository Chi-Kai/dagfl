Synthetic Dataset for Federated Learning.

This document intends to explain the synthetic dataset proposed by [1] in an “understandable” way.

Caldas et al. [1] base their synthetic dataset on Li et al.'s [2] who in turn based their synthetic dataset on Shamir et al’s [3].

For comprehensibility these three datasets are explained in a chronological order: [3], [2], [1]

A. Shamir et al. [3]

Generate nnn i.i.d. (independent and identically distributed - sampled from the same distribution) samples (x,y)(x,y)(x,y).

Input xxx

The input x∈R500x \in \mathbb{R}^{500}x∈R500, is a 500-dimensional vector sampled from x∈N(0,Σ)x \in \mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma)x∈N(0,Σ), where Σj,j=j−1.2\Sigma_{j,j}=j^{-1.2}Σj,j​=j−1.2 is a diagonal covariance matrix. xxx is hence sampled from a multivariate normal distribution with mean 0 and the covariance matrix Σ\SigmaΣ. Σ\SigmaΣ being diagonal (0 everywhere but on the diagonal) makes the (500) dimnesions of the normal distribution linearly independent (each feature in xxx is independent of each other and has hence not any relation to it’s other features) with shrinking variances in the dimensions (the values on the diagonal). The smaller the variance in the normal distirbution the more likely a sample is closer to the mean (here 0).

True Model x→yx \to yx→y

The “true model” x→yx \to yx→y is defined by y=sum(x)+ϵ,ϵ∼N(0,1)y=sum(x)+\epsilon, \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)y=sum(x)+ϵ,ϵ∼N(0,1).
The relationship between each xxx and it’s corresponding label yyy is the sum over all features in x with some gaussian noise (random ϵ\epsilonϵ sampled from a normal distribution with mean 0 and variance 1) added.

B. Li et al. [2]

Li et al. impose additional heterogeneity to [3] to make it more suitable for federated learning. They do this by introducing “devices”. A device differs from other devices in (a) it’s “true model” (b) the cluster (multivariate normal distribution) from which xxx is sampled has a shifted mean.

Generate kkk devices, each with a set of nnn samples (Xk,Yk)(X_k,Y_k)(Xk​,Yk​), with x∈R60x\in\mathbb{R}^{60}x∈R60 and y∈{0,1}10y\in\{0,1\}^{10}y∈{0,1}10.

Input xxx

Sample nnn different xk∼N(vk,Σ)x_k \sim \mathcal{N}(v_k, \Sigma)xk​∼N(vk​,Σ), Σj,j=j−1.2\Sigma_{j,j}=j^{-1.2}Σj,j​=j−1.2 is diagonal (see 1.), vk∼N(Bk,1)v_k \sim \mathcal{N}(B_k,1)vk​∼N(Bk​,1), Bk∼N(0,1)B_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)Bk​∼N(0,1).

For each device kkk:

  1. Sample a 60-dimensional vector BkB_kBk​ from a multivariate normal distribution wit mean 0 and the covariance matrix being an identity matrix (all 0 with 1’s on the diagonal).
  2. Sample a 60-dim. vector vkv_kvk​ from a mult. normal distribution with mean BkB_kBk​ and covariance matrix 1 (as above). Each device now has a vector v_k which is sampled from a similar cluster (mult. norm distr. wit cov. matrix 1) but clustered around a different center (mean).
  3. Sample nnn different 60-dim. xkx_kxk​ from a mult. norm. distr. clustered around vkv_kvk​. The clusters shape is as in A defined by Σ\SigmaΣ. This leads to a device specific set XkX_kXk​ with differing means but from a similarly shaped distribution. When normalized the distribution of XXX across all devices should be approx. equal.

True Model x→yx \to yx→y

The “true models” describing the relationships between xkx_kxk​ and yky_kyk​ for each device kkk is given by
yk=argmax(softmax(Wkxk+bk))y_k = argmax(softmax(W_kx_k + b_k))yk​=argmax(softmax(Wk​xk​+bk​)), Wk∈R10×60W_k \in \mathbb{R}^{10 \times 60}Wk​∈R10×60, bk∈R10b_k \in \mathbb{R}^{10}bk​∈R10.
Where Wk∼N(uk,1)W_k \sim \mathcal{N}(u_k,1)Wk​∼N(uk​,1), bk∼N(uk,1)b_k \sim \mathcal{N}(u_k,1)bk​∼N(uk​,1), uk∼N(0,1)u_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)uk​∼N(0,1).

The true model architecture is an ANN without an activation function with 10 neurons. The output is a 10-dim vector with 0’s and a 1 for the highest number coming from the 10 neurons.
The task is hence a multiclass classification (10 classes). The weight matrix WkW_kWk​ and the bias vector bkb_kbk​ are sampled for each device, such that the true models differ for each device.

For each device kkk:

  1. Sample a 10-dim vector uku_kuk​ from a mult. norm. distr. with mean 0 and an identity cov-matrix (see B.1.).
  2. Sample a 1-dim bias vector bkb_kbk​ from a mult. norm. distr. with mean uku_kuk​ and cov-matrix 1 (as above).
  3. Sample 60 different 10-dim. vectors wkw_kwk​ from a mult. norm. distr. with mean uku_kuk​ and cov-matrix 1 (as above). Glue them together to form the 60×1060 \times 1060×10 weight matrix WkW_kWk​.

This leads to kkk different tasks which weights come from similar distributions (equal in shape, but centered around different means uku_kuk​). This might make the tasks somewhat similar but to different to effectively apply meta-learning. But maybe the introduced heterogeneity is not enough to cripple meta-learning approaches. Surely it mimics the idea of devices differing slightly in their “true model” and their local datasets distributions.

C. Caldas et al. (LEAF) [1]

Caldas et al. take Li et al.'s [2] rules for building a synthetic dataset and add even more rules to increase heterogeneity with the goal to make the tasks so distinct such that common meta-learning approaches would struggle to effectively learn on the synthetic data set. This is supposedly reached by having device specific “true models” (as in B) which are potentially sampled from more than one cluster center (the means from which the model weights are drawn are further apart than in B).

Unfortunately there is a discrepancy between the paper [1] and their published github repository introducing their system for public usage [4].

The math describing the synthetic dataset in the paper [1] doesn’t add up*, hence the following description is based on the code published in [4].
